# Simulation Inputs and Validation Criteria for a Rust + Polars Reimplementation of the Sequential Trial Emulation Data-Expansion Stage

## TL;DR
- The highest-value, directly reusable resources are three open-source R simulators/datasets tied to the in-scope sequential-trial-emulation (STE) design — Keogh et al. 2023 (`ruthkeogh/sequential_trials`), Limozin/Seaman/Su 2025 (`juliettelimozin/Multiple-trial-emulation-IPTW-MSM-CIs`), and the `TrialEmulation` package's own example data/vignettes — which between them supply explicit DGP coefficients, known-truth marginal risk differences, and published operating characteristics (bias, coverage, relative efficiency).
- For your three-tier plan: Tier 1 (bit-exact expansion) is best anchored on the `TrialEmulation` `trial_example`/`data_censored` datasets and the arXiv 2402.12083 worked schema (id/period/eligible/treatment/outcome → expanded trial frames); Tier 2 (coefficient/SE reproduction) on the Limozin and Keogh pipelines; Tier 3 (known-truth recovery) on the Keogh additive-hazard DGP (true treatment effect α_A = −0.04) and the Limozin DGP (true MRD computed by an n = 1,000,000 "very large RCT" Kaplan–Meier).
- Keep clone-censor-weight (CCW) grace-period work (Maringe 2020, Hernán 2016/2017, Gaber 2024) strictly in a sidebar — it is a distinct design with a different expansion (cloning into both arms, not sequential re-enrollment) and should seed a future separate crate, not v1 fixtures.

## Key Findings

1. **Two designs must not be conflated.** Sequential trial emulation (Hernán 2008; Gran 2010; Danaei 2013; Keogh 2023; `TrialEmulation`) expands long person-period data into a *sequence of nested trials*, each starting at a new eligible time origin, with treatment-arm assignment fixed at each trial baseline and artificial per-protocol censoring at deviation, handled by IPCW. Clone-censor-weight (Maringe 2020; Hernán & Robins 2016) instead *clones each person into every strategy-compatible arm at a single time zero*, censoring clones when their data diverge from the assigned strategy, to handle grace periods/immortal time. Your v1 data-expansion target is the former.

2. **Reusable DGPs exist with published coefficients and known truth.** The Keogh 2023 additive-hazard simulator and the Limozin 2025 logistic/Young–Tchetgen simulator both ship runnable R, vary confounding strength / treatment prevalence / event rate, and define ground-truth estimands via large-sample reference datasets. These are tailor-made for Tier 3.

3. **Published operating characteristics give Tier 2/Tier 3 numeric targets**: Keogh 2023 reports bias (×10⁴) and relative efficiency of STE vs MSM-IPTW; Limozin 2025 reports CI coverage across 81 scenarios (n ∈ {200, 1000, 5000}), noting that nominal coverage "was rarely achieved, except at the baseline visit (k=0), and coverage decayed considerably at later visits."

4. **Edge-case literature is rich** (Fu/Hernán time-zero alignment 2025; Hernán et al. 2016 immortal-time; the structural-bias and estimand papers) and should drive adversarial fixtures: repeated eligibility, prevalent-user depletion, ties in event/censor timing, and re-entry across trials.

## Details

### (1) Orientation: the design distinction (and why it matters for expansion code)

**Sequential trial emulation (IN SCOPE).** The canonical lineage is Hernán et al. (2008, *Epidemiology* 19(6):766–779, doi:10.1097/EDE.0b013e3181875e61, PMID 18854702) — the Nurses' Health Study hormone-therapy reanalysis that framed an observational study as "a sequence of trials"; and Gran et al. (2010, *Statistics in Medicine* 29(26):2757–2768, doi:10.1002/sim.4048, PMID 20803557) — the "sequential Cox" approach on the Swiss HIV Cohort, pooling mini-trials via composite likelihood with a stratified weighted Cox analysis. Danaei et al. (2013, *Statistical Methods in Medical Research* 22(1):70–96, doi:10.1177/0962280211403603) applied the pooled-logistic sequential-trials approach to statins and CHD and is the design reference the `TrialEmulation` package follows. The defining data operation: each individual who remains untreated and eligible can enroll as a *control* in successive trials and as a *treated* unit in the trial where they initiate; their earlier-trial rows are artificially censored at the time they deviate. This produces the expanded columns you list (id, trial_period, followup_time, assigned_treatment, treatment, outcome, per-protocol censoring flags).

**Clone-censor-weight grace-period (SIDEBAR, out of scope for v1).** See the CCW section below.

**The package itself.** Su, Rezvani, Gravestock, Chan, et al., "TrialEmulation: An R Package to Emulate Target Trials for Causal Analysis of Observational Time-to-event Data" (arXiv:2402.12083). This is the manuscript describing your Oracle. It documents the data-preparation/expansion step, SIPTCW weighting, the pooled-logistic MSM, and (critically for Tier 1) the worked example data structure: a long dataset keyed on id/period/eligible/treatment/outcome, expanded into per-trial frames where, e.g., an individual eligible at t=0,1,2 who initiates at t=2 appears as control in trials 0 and 1 (censored at t=2) and treated in trial 2. The package is maintained by Isaac Gravestock (Roche) and Li Su (MRC-BSU); newer versions expose the `trial_sequence()` / `set_data()` / `set_switch_weight_model()` / `calculate_weights()` / `expand()` pipeline. A separate, newer package `SEQTaRget` (`SEQuential`, CRAN, dated May 2026) also implements sequential trial emulation and may serve as a second cross-check oracle.

### (2) Simulation-input / DGP papers (reusable parameterizations called out)

**A. Keogh, Gran, Seaman, Davies, Vansteelandt (2023), "Causal inference in survival analysis using longitudinal observational data: Sequential trials and marginal structural models," *Statistics in Medicine* 42(13):2191–2225 (doi:10.1002/sim.9718; arXiv:2110.03117). Open access. Code: github.com/ruthkeogh/sequential_trials.** Provides BOTH a DGP and validation criteria, and it is the single most reusable in-scope simulator because the design under test *is* sequential trials. Verbatim DGP from `dat_sim.R` (main branch), confirmed by direct source read:
- Cohort n = 1000; n.visit = 5 (visits 0–4); 1000 simulated datasets (loop in `analysis_seq.R`).
- Unmeasured U ~ Normal(0, sd 0.1); baseline confounder L₀ ~ Normal(mean = U, sd 1).
- Time-varying confounder AR(1)-type update: `L_k ~ Normal(0.8·L_{k−1} − 1·A_{k−1} + 0.1·(k−1) + U, sd 1)`. The 0.8 is the AR(1) coefficient; lagged-treatment effect −1; deterministic drift +0.1·(k−1).
- Treatment initiation (absorbing once started): `logit P(A_k = 1) = γ₀ + γ_L·L_k`. Scenario 1: γ₀=−1, γ_L=0.5. Scenario 2 (rarer treatment): γ₀=−3, γ_L=0.5. Scenario 3 (strong confounding): γ₀=−1, γ_L=3.
- Additive-hazard outcome within each unit interval: `λ = α₀ + α_A·A_k + α_L·L_k + α_U·U`, with α₀=0.2, **true treatment effect α_A = −0.04**, α_L=0.015, α_U=0.015 (identical across scenarios); event time = −log(U(0,1))/λ truncated into the interval; administrative censoring at t=5.
- True estimand = marginal risk difference (always-treat vs never-treat); true values obtained by a "very large RCT" reference dataset. The estimator is an additive-hazard MSM with stabilized IPACW weights.
- Reported validation outputs: bias in the risk difference (reported ×10⁴ with Monte-Carlo 95% CIs), mean estimated survival curves, and relative efficiency of STE vs MSM-IPTW (ratio of empirical variances). Headline finding: STE tends to involve less extreme weights and is more efficient for the MRD at most follow-up times, occasionally reversed at late times.

**B. Limozin, Seaman, Su (2025), "Inference procedures in sequential trial emulation with survival outcomes: comparing confidence intervals based on the sandwich variance estimator, bootstrap and jackknife," *Statistical Methods in Medical Research* 34(10):2011–2033 (doi:10.1177/09622802251356594; arXiv:2407.08317). Open access. Code: github.com/juliettelimozin/Multiple-trial-emulation-IPTW-MSM-CIs (file `Code/simulate_MSM_simplified.R`).** This team is exactly your project's lineage (MRC-BSU, Li Su). It uses the `TrialEmulation` package's `data_preparation` for expansion and `trial_msm` for fitting, so its pipeline is a near-direct integration-test analog. DGP (Young & Tchetgen Tchetgen 2014 algorithm; from paper Section 5.1.1, Tables 2–3):
- One continuous time-varying confounder X1 plus a time-invariant confounder **X2 ~ N(0,1)**.
- Treatment model (Table 2, verbatim): `logit{Pr(A_tj=1 | A_{t,j−1}, X1_tj, X2, Y_{t,j−1}=0)} = α_a + 0.05·A_{t,j−1} + α_c·X1_tj + 0.2·X2`; treatment is monotone from baseline eligibility.
- Outcome baseline-hazard intercept α_y controls event rate: α_y = −4.7 (low, 5–6.5% events), −3.8 (medium, 10–14%), −3 (high, 20–25%) — corresponding to the three `simulation_*_jackknife.R` scripts.
- Treatment-model intercept α_a controls prevalence: α_a = −1 (low, 25–30% ever-treated), 0 (medium, 50–60%), +1 (high, 75–80%).
- Confounding-strength parameter α_c varied; the low confounding level is **α_c = 0.1** (Table 3); the paper notes α_c had relatively small influence on the DGP.
- Full grid = 81 scenarios = 3 event rates × 3 treatment prevalences × 3 confounding strengths × 3 sample sizes; n ∈ {200, 1000, 5000}; 1000 datasets/scenario; 5 sequential trials emulated (m=0..4), trial 0 the target population.
- Known truth: true MRDs computed by generating two n = 1,000,000 datasets with A forced to all-1 ("always treated") and all-0 ("never treated"), then Kaplan–Meier marginal risks; saved as `true_value_surv1.rda` / `true_value_surv0.rda`.
- Reported validation outputs: empirical CI coverage of the MRD per trial visit for sandwich / nonparametric bootstrap / LEF bootstrap / jackknife. Nominal coverage "was rarely achieved, except at the baseline visit (k=0), and coverage decayed considerably at later visits"; LEF bootstrap was best for small/moderate n, low event rate, low treatment prevalence, while the sandwich estimator was best (and fastest) for large n and medium/high event rates. The repo also contains LEF/jackknife/nonparametric-bootstrap CI code NOT in the package.

**C. Havercroft & Didelez (2012), "Simulating from marginal structural models with time-dependent confounding," *Statistics in Medicine* 31(30):4190–4206 (doi:10.1002/sim.5472, PMID 22826156).** General MSM/IPTW (foundational). The reference algorithm for generating longitudinal survival data so that a marginal structural *logistic* model is correctly specified while exhibiting genuine time-dependent confounding (DAG-based); calibrated to mimic the Swiss HIV Cohort. Full sampling algorithm and proof in appendices; PDF with parameters at people.maths.bris.ac.uk/~maxvd/WH_VD_SIM.pdf. Use it to generate confounding that actually stresses the weighting, and as a cross-check that your expansion + weights recover the IPTW result while naive/adjusted regressions are biased.

**D. Keogh, Seaman, Gran, Vansteelandt (2021), "Simulating longitudinal data from marginal structural models using the additive hazard model," *Biometrical Journal* 63(7):1526–1541 (doi:10.1002/bimj.202000040, PMID 33983641). Code: github.com/ruthkeogh/causal_sim; arXiv:2002.03678.** General MSM. The additive-hazards simulation method that the Keogh 2023 STE DGP builds on (additive conditional hazard → additive MSM, in closed form). Important methodological caveat it documents: Karim et al. (2018) compared the sequential Cox approach with a Cox MSM and wrongly concluded the sequential approach was biased, because they compared a marginal with a conditional estimand and did not ensure both models were correctly specified (Gran & Aalen 2019). This is the cautionary tale for designing your Tier 3 truth.

**E. Young & Tchetgen Tchetgen (2014), "Simulation from a known Cox MSM using standard parametric models for the g-formula," *Statistics in Medicine* 33(6):1001–1014 (doi:10.1002/sim.5994, PMID 24151138).** General MSM. The algorithm the Limozin simulator uses. Lets you simulate so that a Cox MSM, a parametric treatment-given-past model, and a parametric hazard-given-past model are all correctly specified — i.e., isolate confounding bias from misspecification bias. Reports bias of naive vs IPTW estimates (Table I) and CI coverage.

**F. Seaman & Keogh (2024), "Simulating Data From Marginal Structural Models for a Survival Time Outcome," *Biometrical Journal* 66 (doi:10.1002/bimj.70010; arXiv:2309.05025).** General MSM. The most general/recent simulation algorithm (logistic, Cox, or additive-hazards MSM; baseline-covariate-conditional hazards; discrete/continuous treatment), using a copula and the Evans–Didelez frugal parameterization. Reports bias (Table 1) and coverage of naive/sandwich/bootstrap 95% CIs (Table 2). Good for a broad fixture battery.

**G. Other DGP reference points worth knowing:** Xiao, Abrahamowicz & Moodie (2010, *Int J Biostat* 6(2), Article 13) and Young et al. (2010, *Lifetime Data Analysis* 16:71–84) for Cox-MSM simulation; the COVID-19 intermittent-treatment simulation (arXiv:2109.13368) which gives a fully written pseudocode with λ₀=0.005, n=1000, M=100 follow-up, β=(log(3/7), −0.5, −log(1/2), log(3/2)); and the positivity-violation study (arXiv:2403.19606) extending Havercroft–Didelez and Keogh 2021 to strict/near positivity violations — directly useful for adversarial weight-explosion fixtures.

### (3) Validation-criteria / benchmark papers (numbers to check against)

- **`TrialEmulation` example data (Tier 1/2 oracle).** `trial_example` (503 patients) and `data_censored` ship with the package; the Causal-LDA Getting-Started vignette shows exact expansion behavior and prints reference structure. Verbatim console output: the ITT `initiators()` run on `trial_example` produces "Summary of extended data: Number of observations: 1939053"; case-control sampling at `p_control=0.1` yields 99,926 rows. These are your bit-exact targets. Columns: id, eligible, period, outcome, treatment, catvarA/B/C, nvarA/B/C.
- **Limozin 2025**: per-visit MRD coverage tables across 81 scenarios — the cleanest published operating-characteristic grid for STE specifically.
- **Keogh 2023**: bias (×10⁴) and relative-efficiency figures/tables (Figures 4–7, Table 2) — STE vs MSM-IPTW.
- **Karim et al. (2018), "Comparison of statistical approaches dealing with time-dependent confounding in drug effectiveness studies," *Statistical Methods in Medical Research* 27(6):1709–1722.** Compares the sequential Cox and Cox MSM via simulation; useful as a documented *negative* example (flawed comparison) rather than a target to reproduce.

### (4) Edge-case / failure-mode papers for adversarial fixtures (Tier 1)

- **Fu, Harhay, Schneeweiss, Desai, Hernán (2025), "Starting right: aligning eligibility and treatment assignment at time zero when emulating a target trial" (SSRN 5177135).** The definitive recent taxonomy of misalignment; explicitly contrasts sequential trial emulation (for repeated eligibility) vs cloning (for assignment ambiguity). Use its four failure types to build fixtures probing repeated eligibility and re-entry.
- **Hernán, Sauer, Hernández-Díaz, Platt, Shrier (2016), "Specifying a target trial prevents immortal time bias and other self-inflicted injuries in observational analyses," *Journal of Clinical Epidemiology* 79:70–75 (doi:10.1016/j.jclinepi.2016.04.014).** Four canonical emulation failures (E/T0/assignment misalignment).
- **"A structural description of biases that generate immortal time" (PMC11598638)** — formalizes prevalent-user bias / depletion-of-susceptibles when early follow-up is deleted; good for fixtures around baseline-visit events and immortal-time intervals.
- **On estimands in target trial emulation (arXiv:2601.03377)** — distinguishes visit-time vs calendar-time sequential trials; relevant to how you index trial_period.
- **Wiener, Latour, et al. (2026), "Comparing causal estimands from sequential nested versus single point target trials: A simulation study" (arXiv:2601.20725).** Recent Monte-Carlo study (5,000 cohorts × 5,000 people, 3-year follow-up) showing SNT and single-point estimands diverge under effect modification — a known-truth comparator with a fully specified DAG-based DGP.
- **Adversarial structures to encode from these**: ties between event and censoring times; an event exactly at a trial baseline; individuals eligible at every period (maximal row multiplication); re-entry as treated after serving as control; zero-eligible trials; single-row individuals; treatment switching back (as-treated vs per-protocol divergence).

### (5) The clone-censor-weight (CCW) sidebar — DISTINCT design, future separate crate

These provide simulation inputs and validation numbers for a *different* expansion (cloning, not sequential re-enrollment). Do not mix their fixtures into the STE crate.
- **Maringe, Benitez Majano, Exarchakou, Smith, Rachet, Belot, Leyrat (2020), "Reflection on modern methods: trial emulation in the presence of immortal-time bias…," *International Journal of Epidemiology* 49(5):1719–1729 (doi:10.1093/ije/dyaa057).** The CCW tutorial with step-by-step Stata code; lung-cancer surgery example, 6-month grace period. Verbatim benchmark numbers from the abstract: a naïve Kaplan–Meier analysis of the initial cohort "severely overestimated the benefit of surgery on 1-year survival (22% difference)," "as did a survival analysis of the cloned dataset when informative censoring was ignored (17% difference)," whereas the correctly IPC-weighted analysis "still showed evidence of a benefit, though smaller (11% difference)." These three numbers (22% / 17% / 11%) are a clean external validation triple if/when you build the CCW crate.
- **Hernán & Robins (2016), "Using Big Data to Emulate a Target Trial…," *Am J Epidemiol* 183(8):758–764**; **Hernán et al. (2017), "Per-protocol analyses of pragmatic trials," *NEJM* 377(14):1391–1398.**
- **Gaber et al. (2024), "De-Mystifying the Clone-Censor-Weight Method…," *Cancer Medicine* (doi:10.1002/cam4.70461)** and the **"Statistical Methods for Accommodating Immortal Time" review (arXiv:2202.02369)** — the latter gives a permutation-algorithm DGP (following Karim 2016) and directly compares CCW, sequential trials, and PTDM via simulation with known data-generating mechanisms (bias evaluable). This review is the best single bridge if you later want side-by-side STE-vs-CCW benchmarking.
- **"The Complex Estimand of Clone-Censor-Weighting" (arXiv:2404.15073)** — Monte-Carlo true risks at 90 days for various CCW estimands; **arXiv:2605.20003** (treatment-duration CCW). Both have explicit DGPs.

## Recommendations

1. **Stage 0 — lock Tier 1 on the package's own data first.** Build bit-exact fixtures from `trial_example` and `data_censored`, reproducing the published expanded-row counts (1,939,053 ITT rows; 99,926 after p_control=0.1 sampling) before touching any simulated DGP. Benchmark to advance: 100% cell-for-cell match of id/trial_period/followup_time/assigned_treatment/treatment/outcome/censor flags on these two datasets, against a pinned package version.
2. **Stage 1 — port the Keogh 2023 and Limozin DGPs into Rust as fixture generators** using the exact coefficients above (Keogh: AR(1) 0.8, lagged-A −1, drift 0.1, hazard α=0.2/−0.04/0.015/0.015; Limozin: treatment `α_a + 0.05·A_lag + α_c·X1 + 0.2·X2` with α_a∈{−1,0,1}, α_c=0.1 low, X2~N(0,1), event-rate α_y∈{−4.7,−3.8,−3}). Tune γ_L / α_c for confounding strength, α_a for prevalence, α_y for event rate. Benchmark: your Rust-generated long data, when fed to the R Oracle, must expand identically to the R-generated long data expanded by R.
3. **Stage 2 — Tier 2 integration** against the Limozin pipeline (it uses `data_preparation`+`trial_msm`): reproduce log-OR/HR point estimates to within tight numeric tolerance (e.g., |Δβ| < 1e-6 with identical weights; relax to ~1e-3 if you re-fit the GLM) and robust/sandwich SEs to within the documented behavior of the Lin–Wei sandwich estimator.
4. **Stage 3 — Tier 3 known-truth** using Keogh's α_A = −0.04 additive-hazard truth and Limozin's n=1,000,000 KM marginal risks; confirm recovery within Monte-Carlo error over 1000 datasets. Benchmark: bias within the published Monte-Carlo 95% CI, and coverage near nominal in the scenarios where the published studies achieved it (sandwich at large n/high event rate; LEF bootstrap at small n/low event rate). Expect coverage to be good mainly at the baseline visit and to decay at later visits — match that pattern, not a flat 95%.
5. **Adversarial fixtures**: encode the Fu/Hernán misalignment types and the structural edge cases (ties, baseline-visit events, maximal re-eligibility, re-entry as treated). Threshold to change plans: if any adversarial fixture reveals a mismatch with the R Oracle, freeze feature scope and resolve fidelity before proceeding to Tier 2/3.
6. **Defer CCW** to a separate crate; only revisit if/when stakeholders ask for grace-period strategies. The arXiv:2202.02369 review is the entry point, and Maringe 2020's 22%/17%/11% triple is a ready external check.

## Caveats
- Several "results" reported here are operating characteristics from simulation studies, not empirical facts about your data; reproduce the *operating characteristics* (bias within MC error, coverage pattern), not specific point values, where the studies used random seeds you cannot match exactly.
- The Limozin treatment/confounder coefficients are now confirmed from the paper (Table 2: `α_a + 0.05·A_lag + α_c·X1 + 0.2·X2`; X2~N(0,1); α_c=0.1 at the low confounding level); the precise confounder-update rbinom/rnorm literals for X1 and the full α_c grid (the medium/high levels) should still be read directly off `Code/simulate_MSM_simplified.R` to lock exact reproduction.
- `TrialEmulation` is under active development; expansion behavior and default column names can change across versions (the legacy `data_preparation`/`initiators` API vs the newer `trial_sequence()` builder). Pin a specific package version as your Oracle and record its commit/CRAN version. The 1,939,053-row figure is from the current Causal-LDA Getting-Started vignette and should be re-confirmed against your pinned version.
- The Karim 2018 comparison is widely cited but its STE-vs-MSM comparison was methodologically criticized (Gran & Aalen 2019; Keogh 2023); do not treat its numbers as correctness targets.
- Bit-exactness across languages can be defeated by floating-point summation order, tie-breaking in sorts, and GLM solver differences; plan to separate "expansion fidelity" (should be exactly reproducible — integer/category columns) from "estimation fidelity" (tolerance-based).
